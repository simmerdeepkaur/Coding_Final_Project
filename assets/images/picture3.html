A Sacrifice for Truth

With conflicts emerging in different corners of the world, reporters brave enough to fight the power are forced to abandon their motherland and live in exile. Their stories not only represent the setback for their countries but also for journalism and democracy.

Lina Shirzad was a journalist and a voice for women in Afghanistan. She had to give up her aspirations and leave her family after the Taliban assumed control of the country.

Tamerat Feyisa was a journalist in Ethiopia who reported on human rights abuses and corruption in the government. Soon the powerful took notice and Feyisa had to leave his whole life behind to escape death threats. Like many others, he sought refuge in the US.

They are just two of the numerous journalists who went the extra mile to reveal the truth but had to make the ultimate sacrifice for it. In the end, they all wish for the same thing - to go back home.

According to data collected by the Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ), 67 journalists were killed in direct connection to their work and 363 were imprisoned in 2022. This is the highest number since 2018.

Another dataset by CPJ showed that 2,195 journalist executions have been recorded across the globe between 1992-2023.

“Everything that is happening at the moment paradoxically makes the profession (of journalism) all the more important,” said Anna Brakha, a Europe and Central Asia researcher with CPJ.

These journalists come from different countries and political contexts but have met the same fate of either execution or exile.

As Kabul collapsed on August 15, 2021, so did Lina Shirzad’s dreams and goals for her motherland. In the end, she had no option but to start afresh at 27, a life circumstantially chosen for her, in exile. “I did not think that one day Afghanistan would fall, and all my dreams would just remain a dream,” said Shirzad.

Shirzad had been working as a journalist in northern Afghanistan for 13 years. She grew up in Badakhshan and was working in the same province. When her hometown fell, she fled to Kabul and stayed at Afghanistan’s Journalism Protection Committee’s shelter with hundreds of local Journalists. In due course, she came to the United States as part of an evacuation program through the NGO Sayara.
While leaving Kabul, Shirzad was taken to the airport in one of the four minibuses evacuating journalists. As the buses approached closer to the airport, Shirzad could see people spending the night behind the airport gates, desperate for even a sliver of an opportunity to enter the airport and get themselves out of the country.
Shirzad’s eyes were fixated on the window of the bus, but her mind was elsewhere, speeding through the same thoughts over and over again.


“What will I do where I’m going now? Who is there, who am I facing, how will the environment be? What about my goals and dreams?”

Kabul had collapsed quickly and the window of opportunity to escape, for many, was getting smaller. If Shirzad was going to escape, she had to do it now! When she left for Qatar, she could not inform her family about the runoff. During a connecting flight from Qatar to Italy, she called her elder sister to break the news. In the four months that followed, Shirzad’s mother had still not recovered from her daughter’s absence. Her mental conditions worsened, landing her in the hospital. Regardless, there was nothing that could have been done about it.

“If I were there, my family's life would be in danger and I would create problems for them. And I would lose one of my family members due to my presence (in Afghanistan),” Shirzad said.

As a journalist with Voice of America, Shirzad reported on the narcotics beat. Every time she prepared a story, accusations of being an infidel followed. She received threats from various people who disapproved of her working relationship with Americans. The threats became more intense after the Taliban usurpation. Shirzad was explicitly told when the Badakhshan provin fell, she would be executed. The fear in her heart grew stronger, not just for her own, but for her family’s safety. To protect her loved ones, Shirzad had to leave.

“I just want to meet my mom once, and give her a big hug,” she added.

Shirzad now lives in Sacramento, California. She is working as a videographer in a production company and at a restaurant packing food orders. She wishes for two things, to resume her journalism career and to go back home.

Tamerat's Story
Home

Once the USA announced the withdrawal of its troops from Afghanistan, the Taliban launched a paramount offensive, taking over several provincial capitals of the country. To escape their extremist methods, citizens started fleeing to Kabul, the nation’s capital, hoping to find protection from the Islamic fundamentalist group. To their dismay, the Taliban eventually entered Kabul, crumbling Afghan security forces and took control of the presidential palace.
Soldiers abandoned their posts, some surrendered to the Taliban, and the Afghan government collapsed. The 20-year US-led military campaign in Afghanistan officially came to an end. The United States ended its longest war of bloodshed. For Afghanistan, after more than one year of Taliban rule, the country still lives in fear and uncertainty.
Lina Shirzad is one of many journalists who had to flee their country if they wanted a shot at living a decent life, or in some cases, having a life at all. For Shirzad, this decision came at the cost of separating from everyone she loves.


“The moment when I was leaving Afghanistan was very difficult. I shed so many tears that even after all these months, I have not recovered because I left home alone and no one from my family was with me,” she added.
